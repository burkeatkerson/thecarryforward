The [Taxpayer Advocate Service](https://www.taxpayeradvocate.irs.gov) is an independent organization within the IRS — headed by the National Taxpayer Advocate, with at least one office in every state — whose job under Section 7803(c) is to help taxpayers whom the normal process is failing. It is best understood as an internal escalation channel with real procedural teeth: when a refund freeze, a stalled identity-theft case, or an about-to-fire levy is causing genuine harm and ordinary contact has gone nowhere, TAS assigns a human case advocate who can move the machinery. What it is not: a second Appeals office, a settlement program, or a way to change an answer that is merely unwelcome.

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>TAS takes cases involving economic hardship or systemic IRS delay, entered on Form 911, and can order the IRS to stop or take an action through a Taxpayer Assistance Order. It repairs broken process; it cannot change substantive tax outcomes.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## When TAS helps

TAS case-acceptance criteria fall into two families. **Economic burden**: the IRS's action or inaction is causing or about to cause financial harm — a levy that will bounce payroll, a frozen refund the taxpayer needs for rent, mounting costs from IRS delay, or irreparable injury if nothing changes. **Systemic burden**: the IRS has missed its own processing timeframes by 30 days or more, has not responded by a promised date, or a procedural breakdown means the taxpayer's issue simply is not moving. The classic wins are unglamorous: amended returns lost for a year, identity-theft flags that no one clears, lien-certificate applications stalled ahead of a closing (the transaction context described in [federal tax liens explained](/irs-controversy/federal-tax-liens-explained)), and payments applied to the wrong period generating false balance-due notices.

Entry is [Form 911](https://www.taxpayeradvocate.irs.gov), fax or mail to the local office, or a phone intake; congressional offices route constituent tax problems to the same system. Specificity wins: notice numbers, dates, the deadline about to be blown, and the concrete harm.

## The Taxpayer Assistance Order

TAS's formal power, under Section 7811, is the **Taxpayer Assistance Order (TAO)** — a directive that the IRS release a levy, cease a collection action, or act on a stalled matter, issuable where the taxpayer is suffering or about to suffer significant hardship. Only senior IRS officials can override one, in writing. In practice most cases resolve through Operations Assistance Requests — the advocate's routine asks to the responsible IRS function — with the TAO as leverage in the background. TAS involvement does not by itself suspend deadlines: the 30-day [CDP window](/irs-controversy/cdp-hearings-explained), Tax Court petition dates, and refund-claim statutes all keep running, so TAS is pursued *alongside* formal rights, never instead of them.

## What TAS cannot do

**Matching the problem to the right channel saves months.**

| Problem | Right channel |
| --- | --- |
| Disagree with exam adjustments | Protest to [Appeals](/fundamentals/appeals-process-overview) |
| Cannot pay a correct liability | Installment agreement, CNC, or offer — see the [collection defense guide](/irs-controversy/collection-defense-guide) |
| Levy imminent, hearing rights open | CDP request |
| Refund frozen 9 months, no explanation | TAS |
| Levy about to cause eviction despite pending agreement | TAS |
| Penalty you believe is unfair but legally correct | Reasonable cause / abatement request, not TAS |

TAS cannot overrule an examiner or Appeals on the merits, compromise a liability, expedite anything the statute forbids expediting, or handle matters already in litigation. It also is not confidential in the way counsel is — advocates are IRS employees with strong but not absolute confidentiality rules.

<Callout label="Practice note">
  <p>TAS also runs on public reporting: the National Taxpayer Advocate's annual reports to Congress identify the year's worst systemic problems, and the Taxpayer Advocacy Panel channels public suggestions. If your issue is a design flaw rather than a stuck case, the systemic advocacy intake exists for exactly that. Both functions trace to the same source as the <a href="https://www.irs.gov/taxpayer-bill-of-rights">Taxpayer Bill of Rights</a>, which TAS itself championed into IRS policy.</p>
</Callout>
