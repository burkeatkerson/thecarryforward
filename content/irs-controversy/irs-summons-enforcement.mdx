The administrative summons under Section 7602 is where an IRS exam stops being a negotiation. It compels testimony and production of records, and when the recipient balks, the Department of Justice petitions a federal district court for enforcement under standards — from *United States v. Powell*, 379 U.S. 48 (1964) — that the government satisfies with a one-page declaration in nearly every case. Practitioners who treat a summons fight as a serious merits opportunity misread the field: the realistic wins are procedural (Section 7609 notice defects), privilege-based, or achieved earlier, back when the dispute was still an IDR. That earlier stage, including the delinquency-notice and pre-summons steps that precede issuance, is mapped in [IDR strategy and exam management](/irs-controversy/idr-strategy-exam-management).

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>Powell asks only for legitimate purpose, potential relevance, non-possession, and procedural regularity — a burden courts call "slight." Summons defense is really about Section 7609 third-party procedure, privilege assertions document by document, and the rare referral-to-DOJ bar of Section 7602(d).</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## The Powell framework and what actually defeats it

The four Powell elements make a prima facie case the agent's declaration establishes; the burden then shifts to the summoned party to disprove an element or show enforcement would abuse the court's process — issued for harassment, for an improper purpose, or in bad faith. The Supreme Court's gloss in *United States v. Clarke*, 573 U.S. 248 (2014), lets a taxpayer examine IRS officials about motive only on "specific facts or circumstances plausibly raising an inference of bad faith" — a pleading standard few meet. Genuine limits that do exist:

- **Section 7602(d):** no summons may issue, and none may be enforced, while a Justice Department referral for criminal prosecution of that person is in effect.
- **Possession:** material demonstrably already produced defeats the third element — one reason the production log recommended in IDR practice matters.
- **Privilege:** enforcement orders routinely carve out documents withheld under attorney-client privilege, Section 7525, or work product, asserted item by item on a log — the doctrines detailed in [privilege in tax matters](/irs-controversy/privilege-in-tax-matters). Blanket refusals fail; particularized logs get hearings.
- **Fifth Amendment:** available to individuals for testimonial acts, sharply limited for records by the required-records and collective-entity doctrines — corporations have no privilege at all.

## Third-party and John Doe summonses

**The three summons postures carry very different procedural rights.**

| Type | Served on | Taxpayer notice | Taxpayer remedy |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Direct (§7602) | The taxpayer | N/A | Refuse and defend the government's enforcement suit |
| Third-party (§7609) | Bank, accountant, customer, provider | Yes, within 3 days of service | Petition to quash within 20 days; compliance stayed |
| John Doe (§7609(f)) | Recordkeeper re: unnamed class | None (identities unknown) | Ex parte court approval substitutes for notice |

The Section 7609 petition to quash is the one true taxpayer-initiated summons proceeding: 20 days, in the district where the summoned party resides, and it suspends both compliance and — under Section 7609(e) — the statute of limitations, a trade-off to price before filing. Note the exceptions: summonses in aid of *collection* of an assessed liability generally carry no notice or quash rights, which surprises taxpayers whose banks simply comply.

John Doe summonses, requiring ex parte judicial approval, are the instrument behind the offshore-banking cases and the cryptocurrency-exchange customer sweeps; the summoned exchange can resist, but the unnamed customers largely cannot until identified.

## Where a summons fight fits in exam strategy

Non-compliance with an enforcement order is contempt, so the endgame is fixed; the question is what the fight buys. Occasionally: time, a narrowed order, protected documents preserved. Frequently: a district-court record of obstruction that follows the taxpayer into [Appeals](https://www.irs.gov/appeals) and any later proceeding in the [Tax Court](https://www.ustaxcourt.gov) — where, additionally, unagreed exam adjustments arrive with the IRS having had compulsory process and the taxpayer having visibly resisted it. For specialty claims, a summons for credit-study workpapers is the worst posture in which to first consider whether they were privileged; they usually are not, as the R&D-specific discussion in [R&D credit audit defense](/rd-tax-credit/rd-credit-audit-defense) explains.

<Callout label="Practice note">
  <p>The pre-summons letter is the last cheap exit. Once IRS counsel drafts a summons, institutional momentum favors enforcement, and once DOJ files, dismissal without substantial compliance is rare. The economical response to most summons threats is a negotiated production with a privilege log — the same deal that was available months earlier at IDR stage, now on worse terms.</p>
</Callout>
