Circular 230 — formally 31 C.F.R. Part 10 — is the Treasury regulation that governs who may practice before the IRS and how they must behave while doing it. It binds attorneys, CPAs, and enrolled agents; it sets duties of competence and diligence, minimum standards for return positions and written advice, and rules on fees and solicitation; and it gives the IRS Office of Professional Responsibility the power to censure, suspend, or disbar practitioners who violate it. For anyone hiring specialty-tax help, it is the accountability framework the provider either operates inside — or conspicuously does not.

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>Circular 230 requires practitioners to be competent, diligent, and to advance only positions with real support — and it sharply restricts contingent fees, particularly around refund claims. A specialty provider's fee structure tells you whether these rules are constraining them.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## What Circular 230 requires

The operative duties, condensed:

- **Competence (§10.35).** The practitioner must possess, or acquire through study or association, the knowledge and skill the engagement requires. A generalist signing a cost segregation or research credit position is vouching for specialist competence.
- **Diligence and reliance (§10.22).** Due diligence in preparing filings and in determining the correctness of representations made to clients and to the IRS. A practitioner may rely on another's work product only with reasonable care — "the study firm said so" is not, by itself, diligence.
- **Position standards (§10.34).** No signing a return, or advising a position, that lacks a reasonable basis or amounts to a willful understatement. These standards run parallel to the preparer penalties in the Code and the accuracy penalties taxpayers face under Section 6662.
- **Written advice (§10.37).** Advice must be based on reasonable factual and legal assumptions and cannot ignore facts the practitioner knows or should know.
- **Errors and omissions (§10.21).** A practitioner who learns of a client's noncompliance or error must advise the client promptly of the error and its consequences.

Enforcement runs through the Office of Professional Responsibility; the taxpayer-facing counterpart of this framework — what taxpayers themselves are owed in dealings with the IRS — is set out in the [Taxpayer Bill of Rights](https://www.irs.gov/taxpayer-bill-of-rights).

## Why contingent fees around refund claims are restricted

Section 10.27 of Circular 230 generally bars contingent fees for services relating to preparing returns, with narrow exceptions — principally claims or amended returns being examined or challenged by the IRS, penalty and interest disputes, and judicial proceedings. The logic is not subtle: a fee computed as a percentage of the refund pays the practitioner to inflate the refund, precisely the incentive the position standards exist to check.

The specialty-tax industry is where this rule meets practice. "You pay nothing unless we find credits" is a contingent fee on an as-filed claim — a structure the rule was written to prevent, offered routinely by firms whose staff are often not Circular 230 practitioners at all. (A 2014 district court decision, *Ridgely v. Lew*, limited the IRS's authority to police contingent fees for ordinary refund claims prepared by practitioners, which is why the marketing persists; the incentive problem the rule identified is unchanged, and credentialed firms largely still follow it.)

## What this means when vetting providers

| Signal | What it suggests |
| --- | --- |
| Fixed or hourly fee, credentialed signer | Operating inside Circular 230's standards |
| Percentage-of-refund fee | Incentive to inflate; likely outside or testing §10.27 |
| Deliverable is a number, not workpapers | Nothing for your CPA to exercise §10.22 diligence on |
| "Audit defense included" without a named practitioner | Ask who, with what credential, appears before the IRS |

Your own CPA's Circular 230 duties are also in play: they cannot blindly sign a return incorporating a specialty study, so a provider whose workpapers cannot survive your CPA's review is a provider your CPA may refuse to rely on. The full vetting framework — methodology, exam support, fee structure — is in [choosing a specialty tax provider](/fundamentals/choosing-a-specialty-tax-provider); the statutory backdrop for practitioner discipline sits in Title 31 rather than [Title 26](https://uscode.house.gov/browse/prelim@title26&edition=prelim), which is itself a clue that these are professional-conduct rules, not tax computations.

<Callout label="Watch out">
  <p>Circular 230 protects taxpayers only indirectly — it disciplines practitioners, but the tax, penalties, and interest from an inflated claim land on the taxpayer regardless of who prepared it. Hiring a provider outside the regulation's reach does not transfer the risk; it just removes the referee.</p>
</Callout>
