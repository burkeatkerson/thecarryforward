Virginia is one of the clearer illustrations of a design pattern discussed in [how state credits differ](/state-rd-credits/how-state-credits-differ): the state splits its research incentive into a small-company tier that is **refundable** and a large-company tier that is not. The Research and Development Expenses Tax Credit (the "minor" credit) pays cash to companies with modest Virginia research budgets; the Major Research and Development Expenses Tax Credit serves companies spending more than $5 million in Virginia qualified research expenses, nonrefundably. Both are application-based, both draw from annual statewide pools, and both die for the year if the application deadline passes. Rates, caps, and thresholds here are as of mid-2026 and change legislatively — verify with the Virginia Department of Taxation before relying on them.

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>Virginia's minor R&D credit is refundable but small — computed on a capped slice of incremental in-state expenses, subject to an annual statewide pool and a September application deadline. Companies over $5 million in Virginia QREs must use the nonrefundable major credit instead.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## The minor credit: small, but cash

The minor credit is computed on **incremental** Virginia qualified research expenses — the excess over a base amount — at a statutory rate, but only on a capped slice of that increment, so the per-taxpayer credit tops out in the tens of thousands of dollars (as of mid-2026). A higher rate applies where the research is conducted with a Virginia public or private college or university. The credit definitions borrow from federal [Section 41](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/businesses/research-credit), limited to research performed in Virginia.

What makes the minor credit notable is refundability: it pays out even with zero Virginia tax liability. A pre-revenue startup with a Virginia lab files the application and receives a check. The statewide pool is modest — in the single-digit millions — and if aggregate approved requests exceed it, every claimant is prorated down.

## The major credit: bigger base, no refund

Companies with Virginia QREs above **$5 million** claim the major credit instead: a percentage (10% as of mid-2026) of the excess of current-year Virginia QREs over half of the prior-three-year average — an ASC-style computation. It is nonrefundable, limited to a percentage of the taxpayer's liability for the year, with excess carrying forward. It has its own, larger statewide pool, also subject to proration.

**The table summarizes the two tiers as of mid-2026; figures are approximate and legislatively adjusted — confirm current amounts with the state.**

| Feature | Minor credit | Major credit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Who | Virginia QREs ≤ statutory threshold | Virginia QREs > $5 million |
| Computation | Rate on capped incremental slice; university-research bump | 10% of excess over 50% of 3-year average |
| Refundable | Yes | No — liability limit plus carryforward |
| Statewide cap | Yes, prorated if oversubscribed | Yes, larger pool, prorated |
| How claimed | Application to Department of Taxation by annual deadline | Same |

## Mechanics, fit, and the trap

Both credits run on an application to the [Virginia Department of Taxation](https://www.tax.virginia.gov) filed after the credit year closes — historically due September 1 — with the department certifying each taxpayer's allocation before the credit goes on the return. This is not a self-assessed credit: the return claim follows the certification, and amended-return strategies that work federally do not work here.

The minor credit fits startups and small firms cleanly — refundable, simple, worth claiming even at small dollar amounts since the application burden is light once federal workpapers exist with Virginia sourcing. The major credit fits established companies with concentrated Virginia research; its liability limit means loss-year claimants accrue carryforwards rather than cash.

The trap is the **deadline-and-pool structure**. Unlike the federal credit, there is no claiming Virginia's credit on a late or amended return after the application window closes — the year's benefit is simply forfeited, and even timely applicants can be prorated below their computed amount. Calendar the application separately from the return. Companies weighing where a marginal research dollar lands might compare Virginia's capped refundability with the uncapped refundable regimes described in [refundable and transferable state credits](/state-rd-credits/refundable-transferable-state-credits), or with neighboring [North Carolina](/state-rd-credits/north-carolina-rd-tax-credit), which no longer offers a general credit at all.
