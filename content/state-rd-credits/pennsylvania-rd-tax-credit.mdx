Pennsylvania's research credit is the canonical application-based, transferable state credit. As of mid-2026, the design: 10% of Pennsylvania qualified research expenses over a base amount — 20% for qualifying small businesses — awarded through an annual application to the Department of Revenue from a statutorily capped pool with a small-business set-aside, prorated when oversubscribed. And its signature feature: awarded credits a taxpayer cannot use may be **sold** to other Pennsylvania taxpayers. Pool size, rates, and deadlines are legislative variables; confirm the current cycle with the [Pennsylvania Department of Revenue](https://www.revenue.pa.gov).

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>Pennsylvania's credit is won by application, not computed on a return: 10% (20% small business) of incremental in-state QREs from a capped, prorated annual pool. Unused credits are sellable — real cash for loss companies — but a missed application deadline forfeits the year entirely.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## Computation and eligibility

The definitional layer borrows from federal [Section 41](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/businesses/research-credit): qualified research and QREs take their federal meaning, restricted to research performed in Pennsylvania. The credit applies to the excess of the year's Pennsylvania QREs over a base derived from prior-year in-state spending, so the incremental logic — and the need for sourced historical data — carries over from the [general state-credit framework](/state-rd-credits/how-state-credits-differ). The small-business tier (20%) turns on a statutory size test measured by assets; entities of essentially any form may apply, and credits awarded to pass-throughs can flow to owners against personal income tax under the statute's pass-through rules.

## The pool, the proration, and the award

**The pipeline is the design — each stage can shrink the number, as of mid-2026:**

| Stage | What happens | What can go wrong |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Application (statutory fall deadline) | File with the Department of Revenue for the prior tax year's QREs | Miss the date, lose the year |
| Review | Department verifies computation and compliance (tax clearance matters) | Delinquencies can block an award |
| Award from pool | Capped annual appropriation, small-business set-aside | Oversubscription → proration below the computed amount |
| Use or sell | Offset PA tax per statutory limits, carry forward, or sell | Sale requires approval; discounts to face value |

The pool is the feature practitioners from self-serve states underestimate. A correctly computed $100,000 credit is an *application* for $100,000; the award depends on the year's total demand against the appropriation, and heavy oversubscription has historically produced meaningful proration. Forecast the award, not the computation.

## Selling the credit

Transferability is Pennsylvania's answer to refundability. A holder that cannot use an awarded credit — the common case: a loss-running technology or life-sciences company — may apply to sell or assign it. Sales run at a discount to face value (market pricing varies by year and buyer appetite), buyers take the credit subject to usage limitations, and the Department must approve the transfer. The seller's discount is the cost of converting a paper credit to cash now; for a company with no Pennsylvania tax on the horizon, a discounted sale usually beats an aging carryforward. Federal tax treatment of the sale proceeds is its own analysis — sellers generally recognize income or gain on the sale — and belongs in the model before the decision, a point developed in the [multistate strategy guide](/state-rd-credits/multistate-rd-credit-strategy).

## Who benefits

Three profiles do well: small businesses hitting the 20% tier; loss companies that monetize through sale; and steady Pennsylvania research operations for whom the annual application becomes routine. The credit is less attractive for marginal out-of-state claimants — a thin Pennsylvania QRE sliver must clear an application process, possible proration, and sale friction before producing cash, and the fixed effort is higher than in piggyback states like New Jersey.

## The trap: the calendar, again

Everything in Pennsylvania keys off the application deadline — a fixed statutory date each year, independent of return due dates and extensions. There is no relief for late applications, no claiming the credit directly on a return, and no second window. The secondary trap is tax compliance status: the Department checks that applicants are current on state tax obligations, and delinquencies discovered at award time are expensive. Put the deadline on the calendar in January, reconcile the state accounts before applying, and treat the award letter — not the spreadsheet — as the asset.
