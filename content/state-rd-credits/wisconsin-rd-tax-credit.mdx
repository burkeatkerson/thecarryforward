Wisconsin's research credit is the closest thing among the states to a scaled copy of the federal alternative simplified credit: **5.75%** of Wisconsin qualified research expenses exceeding **50% of the prior three years' average** Wisconsin QREs, with elevated rates — roughly double — for qualified research relating to internal combustion engines and certain energy-efficient products (as of mid-2026). Its second distinguishing feature arrived more recently: the credit is **partially refundable**, with the legislature ratcheting the refundable share of unused credit upward over the years, most recently to as much as 25%. Rates and the refundable percentage move; verify current law with the Wisconsin Department of Revenue.

## Federal math, Wisconsin inputs

The formula will look familiar to anyone who has computed the federal ASC under [Section 41](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/businesses/research-credit): current-year QREs minus 50% of the trailing three-year average, times the rate. Wisconsin borrows the federal definitions of qualified research and qualified research expenses wholesale — four-part test, wage/supply/contract categories — and restricts them to research performed in Wisconsin. If a company has no Wisconsin QREs in the base years, a reduced-rate computation applies to current spending, again mirroring the federal ASC's no-history rule.

The industry-specific rates are a Wisconsin signature: qualified research related to the design and manufacture of internal combustion engines (including hybrid drives) and certain energy-efficient lighting, building-automation, and power-supply products earns roughly double the general rate — a nod to the state's engine and controls manufacturers.

## The partial refund

Unused credit does not simply carry forward. After the credit offsets the year's franchise or income tax, Wisconsin refunds a **percentage of the unused remainder** — a share the legislature has increased in steps, reaching up to 25% for recent years — with the balance carrying forward (15 years under the longstanding rule). For a loss-stage company earning a $200,000 credit, that recent design means roughly $50,000 cash now and $150,000 banked; not [Iowa](/state-rd-credits/iowa-rd-tax-credit)-grade refundability, but meaningfully better than the nonrefundable norm. Where this places Wisconsin in the national cash-out landscape is covered in [refundable and transferable state credits](/state-rd-credits/refundable-transferable-state-credits).

## Fit, mechanics, and the trap

Wisconsin's claimants look like Wisconsin's economy: engine and powertrain manufacturers, controls and instrumentation firms, paper and materials science, medical imaging, and a growing software sector. The ASC-style formula favors growing programs but — because the base is only half the trailing average — pays even steady spenders something. Claims are self-assessed on the return with the research credit schedule; the [Wisconsin Department of Revenue](https://www.revenue.wi.gov) publishes forms and the current refundable percentage. Pass-through entities compute the credit at the entity level with credits flowing to owners under Wisconsin's rules.

The trap is **assuming the federal numbers transfer whole**. The formula is federal in shape, but every input must be Wisconsin-sourced — current QREs and all three base years — and companies with shifting footprints (a Chicago engineering office, remote hires post-2020) routinely overstate the Wisconsin base by importing federal totals. The three-year lookback also means today's sourcing sloppiness corrupts three future claims.
