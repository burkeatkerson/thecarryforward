A property tax appeal contests one thing: the assessor's opinion of value. The tax rate is untouchable — set by school boards and municipalities — but the **assessed value** is an administrative estimate, generated by mass-appraisal models, and it is wrong in individual cases as a matter of statistical necessity. The appeal process exists because of that necessity, it is used by a small fraction of owners, and it runs on deadlines measured in weeks from the notice date.

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>Appeals argue value, classification, or uniformity within a short window after the assessment notice — typically 30 to 90 days. Income properties argue the income approach with real operating data; and where a cost segregation study exists, its component detail can identify personal property being double-taxed and support obsolescence adjustments.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## Assessment ratios: reading the notice correctly

Most jurisdictions do not tax full market value. The assessor estimates market value, applies a statutory **assessment ratio** (100% in some states, 10–40% in others, often varying by property class), and taxing bodies apply millage to the result. The first analytical step is converting the assessment back to the implied market value — a $150,000 assessment at a 25% ratio asserts a $600,000 property. Owners routinely misread a below-market *assessment* as a bargain when the implied market value is far above what the property would sell for. Ratios, class definitions, and exemptions (homestead, agricultural, abatements) are state law and change; verify with the state revenue department and county assessor rather than relying on any general description — the same verify-locally discipline the site applies to [state credit regimes](/rd-tax-credit/state-rd-credits-overview).

## The three valuation approaches

Appeal boards hear the same three approaches appraisers use, weighted by property type:

**Which valuation approach carries which property.**

| Approach | Method | Strongest for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sales comparison | Adjusted comparable sales | Homes, condos, small residential |
| Income capitalization | NOI ÷ capitalization rate | Rentals, offices, retail, multifamily |
| Cost | Replacement cost − depreciation + land | New, special-purpose, rarely-traded property |

For income property, the income approach usually decides the case: actual rent rolls, vacancy, operating expenses, and a supportable cap rate. A building whose NOI fell — lost anchor tenant, deferred maintenance, submarket vacancy — often carries an assessment still tracking a stale model, and the owner's own operating statements are the best evidence in the room. Uniformity arguments (your assessment ratio exceeds comparable properties') are a recognized second front in many states.

## Where cost segregation data helps

An owner who commissioned a [cost segregation study](/cost-segregation/what-is-cost-segregation) holds a component-level inventory of the property, and it serves two appeal purposes. First, **misclassification**: real property tax should fall on real property, and in states that separately tax (or exempt) business personal property, a study's segregation of [equipment-like components](/cost-segregation/five-seven-fifteen-year-examples) from the building can show the real property assessment is capturing value that belongs on the personal property roll — or is exempt outright. Second, **cost-approach evidence**: the study's engineering take-offs and component ages support depreciation and functional-obsolescence adjustments with more granularity than the assessor's mass model. Two cautions keep this honest: income tax classifications do not bind property tax law, which draws its own fixture lines state by state; and the study's numbers cut both ways — a study showing recent renovation costs can just as easily support a *higher* assessment, so review before submitting.

## Process, posture, and when not to bother

The typical ladder: informal assessor review, then the county board of appeals or equalization, then state-level tribunal or court — with evidence standards rising at each rung. Deadlines are jurisdictional and unforgiving. Appeals are generally low-risk, but boards in most jurisdictions *can* raise a value the evidence shows is too low, so an under-assessed owner should leave well alone. Skip the appeal when the overassessment is small against the filing effort, when the assessment lags a rising market (winning this year's fight invites next year's reassessment), or when an abatement agreement or recent purchase price effectively fixes the value. For income property, remember the deduction feedback: property tax is deductible against rental income under [Publication 527](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p527), so a $10,000 assessment saving nets less after the lost deduction — real money still, but budget the fee against the after-tax figure across the years the reduction will hold.
