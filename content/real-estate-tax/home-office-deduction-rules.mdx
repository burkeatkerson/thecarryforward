The home office deduction is narrower than its reputation. Section 280A(c) allows it only for a space used **exclusively and regularly** for business, and only if that space is the taxpayer's **principal place of business** (or a place for meeting clients, or a detached structure). It belongs to the self-employed: employees lost the deduction with the TCJA's suspension of miscellaneous itemized deductions, and the 2025 legislation made the suspension permanent — a remote W-2 job creates no deduction, only the possibility of an employer reimbursement under an accountable plan.

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>Exclusive use is the gate — a desk in the guest room fails, a dedicated room qualifies. Self-employed taxpayers then choose annually between $5 per square foot (max $1,500, no recapture) and actual expenses with depreciation, which is worth more but claws part of itself back at 25% when the house sells.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## Who qualifies?

Three tests, all statutory. **Exclusive use**: the space serves no personal function — the standard is strict, and mixed-use rooms fail outright (narrow exceptions exist for inventory storage and daycare). **Regular use**: continuing, not occasional. **Principal place of business**: after Congress overruled *Soliman* in 1997, Section 280A(c)(1) includes a space used for administrative or management activities when there is no other fixed location where the taxpayer substantially performs them — the provision that qualifies contractors, consultants, and landlords who run operations from home. For a self-managed rental portfolio, the office is also part of the fact pattern supporting trade-or-business status for [QBI purposes](/real-estate-tax/qbi-deduction-rental-real-estate) and hours for [material participation logs](/real-estate-tax/passive-activity-rules-complete) — the deduction itself is often the least of its uses.

## Simplified versus actual: the annual choice

**The two methods compared for a 240-square-foot office in a 2,400-square-foot home.**

| Attribute | Simplified (Rev. Proc. 2013-13) | Actual expense (§280A(c)) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Computation | $5/sq ft, 300 sq ft cap | 10% of utilities, insurance, repairs, etc. |
| Maximum | $1,500 | No cap (limited to business income) |
| Depreciation | None allowed | 39-year on office share of home basis |
| Recapture on sale | None | Yes — unrecaptured §1250, up to 25% |
| Recordkeeping | Square footage only | Full expense file and basis records |
| Election | Annual, either direction | Annual |

The actual method typically wins on dollars — 10% of a real house's carrying costs exceeds $1,500 — at the price of records and future recapture. Depreciation under the actual method uses the office's share of the home's basis (allocated between [land and building](/real-estate-tax/depreciation-land-allocation), as ever) over 39 years as nonresidential property, reported through [Form 4562](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-4562) under [Publication 946](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p946) rules. Either way, the deduction cannot exceed the business's income under Section 280A(c)(5); the excess carries forward — but only under the actual method.

## The recapture at sale

Depreciation is the method choice's long tail. When the home sells, Section 121's exclusion (up to $250,000/$500,000) covers the gain — including the office, so long as it is within the same dwelling unit (Treas. Reg. §1.121-1(e) stopped fragmenting the exclusion in 2002) — **except** for depreciation claimed after May 6, 1997, which is taxed as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at up to 25% under Section 121(d)(6). Ten years of $800 annual office depreciation is $8,000 of gain taxed at sale regardless of the exclusion. The mechanics mirror the site's fuller treatment of [depreciation recapture](/cost-segregation/depreciation-recapture-explained). The deduction-now-versus-recapture-later trade usually still favors deducting — ordinary-rate savings now against 25%-capped tax later — but taxpayers who hate the ledger burden take the simplified method and skip the whole question.

<Callout label="Watch out">
  <p>Failing to claim depreciation does not avoid recapture under the actual method: Section 1250 recapture applies to depreciation "allowed or allowable." Taxpayers using actual expenses should claim the depreciation they will be charged with anyway. The simplified method is the only clean opt-out — its depreciation deemed zero by revenue procedure.</p>
</Callout>
