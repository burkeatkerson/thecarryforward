Real estate professional (REP) claims are among the most frequently litigated issues in the Tax Court, and the examinations follow a script. The examiner is testing two numbers — **750 hours** in real property trades or businesses, and **more than half** of all personal-service hours — plus material participation in the rentals themselves, and the evidence is almost always a log the taxpayer produced after the audit letter arrived. The government wins most of these cases not on law but on arithmetic and credibility.

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>REP exams are document exams: a contemporaneous hour log, a filed grouping election, and a story consistent with the taxpayer's W-2 employment. The recurring fatal errors are full-time outside jobs that make the more-than-half test impossible, spouses combining hours for the qualification tests, and reconstructed logs padded with implausible detail.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## What the examiner tests, in order

**The examination sequence for a Section 469(c)(7) claim.**

| Step | Question | Where claims fail |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Does one spouse have 750+ hours in real property trades or businesses? | Reconstructed logs; investor-type hours counted |
| 2 | Is that more than half of that spouse's total working hours? | Full-time W-2 job outside real estate ends the claim |
| 3 | Was the §1.469-9(g) grouping election filed? | Never filed; material participation then tested per property |
| 4 | Material participation in the rental(s)? | Property-manager-run portfolios; hours mostly the manager's |
| 5 | Do the losses survive recomputation? | §469 disallowance, plus accuracy penalties |

Step 2 is the silent killer. A taxpayer with a 2,000-hour outside job needs more than 2,000 real estate hours — nearly 40 a week on top of full-time work — and the Tax Court reads that arithmetic without sympathy. Hours as an employee of someone else's real estate business only count with 5%-plus ownership. And hours must be in *work*: education, research on prospective purchases, and investor-level review are excluded, while travel hours have fared inconsistently.

## The log: contemporaneity in practice

Treas. Reg. §1.469-5T(f)(4) allows participation to be established "by any reasonable means" — appointment books, calendars, narrative summaries. But the case law has converted that leniency into a trap for reconstruction: courts describe post-hoc logs as "ballpark guesstimates" and discount them wholesale when entries are uniform, rounded, in conflict with other records, or physically implausible (hours claimed while demonstrably at the W-2 job, or logs totaling more waking hours than exist). What survives scrutiny looks like a business record: dated entries made in real time, specific tasks, property identified, corroborated by emails, invoices, mileage, and bank activity. The same evidentiary discipline the site prescribes generally in [audit-ready documentation](/fundamentals/audit-readiness-documentation) applies here at full strength — this is the single most fact-intensive claim most individual returns ever carry.

<Callout label="Watch out">
  <p>The grouping election under Treas. Reg. §1.469-9(g) cannot be made for the first time on audit. A REP with five rentals and no election must prove material participation in each property separately — usually impossible — and courts have enforced the omission even where qualification was otherwise clear. Rev. Proc. 2011-34 permits certain late elections; check it before conceding.</p>
</Callout>

## Spouses, and how the aggregation rules actually run

Joint filers stumble on an asymmetry. For the two *qualification* tests — 750 hours, more-than-half — **one spouse must qualify alone**; the statute says so expressly (Section 469(c)(7)(B), flush language). For *material participation* in the rentals, spousal hours **do** combine under Section 469(h)(5). So a non-working spouse with 800 documented real estate hours qualifies the couple even if the working spouse has none — but a couple with 400 hours each qualifies no one. Returns prepared with the tests blended draw the adjustment automatically.

The stakes usually trace back to depreciation: the REP claim exists to unlock losses a [cost segregation study](/cost-segregation/cost-seg-passive-loss-limits) created, and the interlocking hour tests and election mechanics are laid out in [the grouping election and REP status](/cost-segregation/grouping-election-rep-status), with the full Section 469 architecture in [the passive activity guide](/real-estate-tax/passive-activity-rules-complete). A disallowed claim rarely ends at tax due — a six-figure loss with a guesstimate log invites the 20% accuracy penalty, and [Publication 925](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p925)'s plain statement of the tests undercuts any claimed ignorance. The planning conclusion is unglamorous: qualify on paper in January, log in real time all year, file the election, and the audit becomes an exchange of exhibits rather than a credibility contest.
