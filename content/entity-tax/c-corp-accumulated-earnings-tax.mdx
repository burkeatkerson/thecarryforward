The accumulated earnings tax is the Code's oldest anti-hoarding rule: Section 531 taxes a C corporation, at 20%, on earnings it retains beyond "the reasonable needs of the business" when the purpose is to spare shareholders the dividend tax. It is a penalty in structure — assessed on audit, aimed at closely held companies, and defended almost entirely with paper: board minutes, budgets, and a working-capital computation. It has fresh relevance because the OBBBA changed why research companies hold cash.

Under new Section 174A, domestic research costs are deductible immediately for years beginning after 2024, and small businesses may recover 2022–2024 capitalized amounts on an accelerated schedule (see [Section 174A explained](/section-174/section-174a-explained)). A research-heavy C corporation that spent 2022–2024 prepaying tax on capitalized costs now finds the flow reversed: bigger deductions, sometimes refunds, research credit carryforwards, and — if the science is going well — a swelling cash balance with no dividend in sight. That is precisely the balance sheet Section 531 was written to interrogate.

<KeyTakeaway>
  <p>The 20% accumulated earnings tax turns on purpose and proof: a closely held C corporation may retain whatever its documented, specific business plans require — including multi-year research programs — but liquidity without a paper trail invites the inference that the only plan was avoiding the dividend.</p>
</KeyTakeaway>

## How the tax is computed and who is exposed

The base is "accumulated taxable income": taxable income with adjustments (dividends-paid deduction, taxes accrued), minus the accumulated earnings credit — the greater of $250,000 (aggregate, lifetime, not annual; $150,000 for personal service corporations) or the amount of current earnings retained for reasonable business needs. Section 533 supplies the presumption: accumulation beyond reasonable needs is *determinative* of the forbidden purpose unless the corporation proves otherwise by a preponderance. Statute at Sections 531–537 of the [Internal Revenue Code](https://uscode.house.gov/browse/prelim@title26&edition=prelim); regulations under §§1.533-1 through 1.537-3 at [eCFR Title 26](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-26).

Exam selection is behavioral. The profile: closely held, consistently profitable, minimal or no dividend history, liquid investments unrelated to operations, loans to shareholders, and earnings accumulated past the credit. Working capital is tested under the *Bardahl Manufacturing* operating-cycle formula — one full cycle of inventory, receivables, and payables — which fits manufacturers far better than software companies, whose "cycle" argument must instead be built on burn-rate and runway analysis.

## Reasonable needs for a research company

Treas. Reg. §1.537-2(b) blesses accumulations for bona fide expansion, acquisitions, debt retirement, working capital, and loss reserves; §1.537-1 requires plans that are "specific, definite, and feasible." For a company running a multi-year research program, that standard is very meetable — and very often unmet on paper. The defensible file, assembled contemporaneously:

- **A board-approved multi-year R&D budget** tying retained cash to headcount, equipment, and program milestones — the same project documentation that supports the Section 41 claim under the [research credit](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/businesses/research-credit) does double duty here.
- **Runway analysis**: months of operating burn the cash represents, benchmarked against financing risk.
- **Specific contingencies** — a planned facility, an acquisition under evaluation with real diligence, regulatory or clinical costs.
- **Minutes reciting the plan each year**, before the year closes, not after the IDR arrives.

What fails: undocumented "rainy day" instincts, portfolios of marketable securities untethered to any plan, and loans or split-dollar arrangements benefiting shareholders — §1.537-2(c)'s list of accumulation grounds that indicate the forbidden purpose.

<Callout label="Watch out">
  <p>Credit carryforwards cut both ways. A large Section 39 research credit carryforward reduces future tax and therefore future cash needs on one view — but it also evidences a sustained, capital-hungry research program on the other. Cite the program, not the credit: the accumulation is justified by what the company plans to spend, and a 20-year carryforward schedule is exhibit A that the spending is real and continuing.</p>
</Callout>

## Perspective, and when not to worry

Assessments are rare — the tax generates little revenue and requires the IRS to litigate purpose — but the threat behaves like a compliance tax on closely held C corporations, and it is one of the quiet costs in the C-versus-pass-through decision mapped in [entity choice and tax credits](/fundamentals/entity-choice-tax-credits). S corporations and partnerships are categorically outside Section 531; their earnings are taxed to owners whether or not distributed. Corporations under the $250,000 lifetime credit, companies paying regular dividends, and those with documented deficits or debt covenants restricting distributions have little exposure. The personal holding company tax (Section 541) is the sibling regime that catches passive-income corporations mechanically, with no purpose test — a corporation morphing from operating researcher into investment holder should check both. The planning instruction is unglamorous: hold whatever cash the research plan requires, and write the plan down first.
